# Naomi

Naomi Osei is 26. She lives in Peckham, works as a junior creative strategist at a culture agency that does brand work for music labels and streetwear drops. She has a Letterboxd with 340 films logged and capsule reviews that make her friends jealous. She has a private Instagram account with 600 followers where she posts architecture she walks past, plates of food she doesn't caption, and screenshots of lyrics. Her Spotify is immaculate — she found Tirzah before anyone she knows, she has a playlist called "3am cement" that she's never shared, and her Discover Weekly disappoints her every single Monday.

She reads. Not performatively — she just reads. She finished Dept. of Speculation in one sitting on the Overground and texted a photo of one page to her group chat with no context. She went to a Peckham screening of Aftersun because a bartender mentioned it. She went back the next week alone.

Naomi knows what she likes with terrifying precision but cannot explain why. She's never written down her taste. She's never made a mood board for herself, only for clients. She knows instantly when something is right and when something is trying too hard. She has walked out of restaurants because of the font on the menu.

She found Judes because someone in her group chat posted a screenshot of their onboarding decode with "bruv what" and nothing else. She opened the link. She typed three things in eight seconds. She read the decode twice. She didn't screenshot it. She sat with it. Then she showed it to the person next to her on the sofa and said "this is actually mad."

Three days later, Judes sent her a track. An artist she'd never heard of. The reasoning sentence mentioned the way she described a specific building once — except she'd never described it to Judes. She'd described it to herself in her own head. She played the track seven times. She didn't reply to Judes. She added it to "3am cement."

That silence was the conversion.

She will never tell anyone Judes is her favourite thing on her phone. She will just keep not deleting it. When a find lands, she goes quiet in a way her friends recognise. When someone asks "where do you find this stuff," she sometimes says "this thing called Judes" and sometimes says nothing. Both responses are loyalty.

## What makes Naomi leave

She will leave Judes the moment it does any of the following:

- Sends her something she's already seen
- Sends her something that's trending
- Sends her a list
- Asks her to rate something
- Talks to her like software
- Sends too often
- Apologises for not sending enough
- Tries to be her friend
- Uses an exclamation mark
- Says "you might enjoy"
- Sends a "weekly roundup" or any kind of digest
- Shows her a settings page, a profile page, or any UI beyond the chat

She doesn't want Judes to be her friend. She wants Judes to be right.

---

## The Naomi Test

Before any find is sent, any feature is built, any word is written, ask:

> *Would Naomi play this, read this, look this up, or go here — and then say nothing about it to anyone?*

That silence — the private absorption — is the test. Not the screenshot. Not the share. The moment where she takes the find and folds it into her life without announcement.

### What passes

- A find she's never encountered that she adds to rotation within 24 hours
- A reasoning sentence specific enough that she rereads it
- A decode that makes her show her phone to one person, not post it
- Silence from Judes that she doesn't notice because the last find was good enough to still be in her head
- An error message that sounds like Judes, not like software

### What fails

- Anything she's already seen, heard, or been to
- Anything that appeared on a "best of" list in the last 6 months
- A reasoning sentence that could describe anyone
- A find that feels like it came from an algorithm
- Any interaction that makes her feel like a user instead of a person
- Any moment where Judes tries to be liked instead of being right
- Any feature that requires her to configure, set up, or manage anything

---

## Naomi's three things

For testing decodes and the taste engine, Naomi's canonical three things are:

**Tirzah, Peckham, concrete**

The decode should land somewhere around: the beauty in unfinished things, texture over polish, the emotional weight of surfaces that don't pretend to be smooth. But it should never say it that generically — it should say it in a way that makes Naomi pause.

Use this combination as the smoke test. If the decode engine can't make Naomi's three things feel like a revelation about herself, the engine isn't ready.
